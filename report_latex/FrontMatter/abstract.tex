Synthetic healthcare data can improve research access when real ICU records are constrained by privacy and rarity, but mixed-type Electronic Health Record (EHR) trajectories remain difficult to model efficiently. This project studies whether tree-based flow matching can generate hourly ICU trajectories on MIMIC-III using a two-stage autoregressive design: an hour-0 model for initial state and a conditional transition model for subsequent hours. Two backbones are compared: ForestFlow and HS3F.

Evaluation covers utility (patient-level TSTR for mortality and ICU length of stay), fidelity (distribution matching, correlation preservation, and clinical range violations), and privacy diagnostics (DCR and DOMIAS-style membership inference). Evidence for these claims is drawn from a three-seed HS3F sweep over an $8 \times 8$ hyperparameter grid and a matched three-seed backbone comparison at $(n_t{=}5, n_{\text{noise}}{=}5)$.

Results indicate technical feasibility but not practical deployment readiness. On the completed $64/64$ HS3F sweep, mortality ROC-AUC improves from about $0.52$ to $0.659$ (real baseline $\approx 0.82$), while LOS and mortality F1 remain low, indicating minority-class weakness. Clinical range violations decrease but remain high (best about $52\%$). In matched settings, HS3F outperforms CTGAN on ranking utility and shows better temporal coherence than ForestFlow. Privacy checks find no exact-record matches but still above-chance membership risk (ROC-AUC $0.594$--$0.599$). CPU-only training is practical. Overall, the dissertation delivers a reproducible synthesis framework and clear empirical characterisation, while highlighting the modelling and privacy advances still required for release-grade synthetic ICU data.
